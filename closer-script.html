3 Types of Insurance on a Home
"When you close on a home, there are three types of insurance that come into play:"
"Number one — PMI. Private Mortgage Insurance. Required by the bank if you put less than 20% down. Does absolutely nothing for you or your family — it only protects the bank."
"Number two — Homeowners insurance. Protects the physical structure — fire, flood, storm damage."
"Number three — and the most important — Mortgage Protection. This is the coverage that protects you and your family if you become sick, disabled, or pass away. It's what ensures your family can stay in the home no matter what."
"Any questions on that?"
3 Required Pieces — Plant Now, Ask Later
"Mortgage protection isn't something you can just sign up for anytime like car insurance. You have to qualify for it. Once we go to submit, there are three pieces of information required:"
"First — driver's license. Confirms your identity."
"Second — your Social. That's how they check your prescriptions and medical history through the MIB — the Medical Information Bureau. Primary factor in whether you're approved or declined."
"Third — bank statement or voided check. Confirms no prior insurance fraud and that's how the premium gets paid once approved."
"All of that good?"
SSN plant — say once, casually: "Some people get a little jumpy about the Social — totally normal. That's standard on every insurance application. It's how they pull your medical records and verify your identity. Nothing unusual about it."

"The Price Is Too High" — 4-Step (run in order)
Don't reassure — diagnose. Walk these four in order. Each step gets a small yes that boxes the objection down to the only thing it really is: the number.
Step 1 · Rule out confusion
"Totally get it, [NAME] — most of my clients felt the same. Before we go further: is everything about how this actually works clear to you? Or is there a piece you want me to run through again?"
If confusion → clear it up, then jump to Step 3. If clear → Step 2.
Step 2 · Re-confirm the why (their words)
"Good — then let's go back to why we're here. We're still making sure [BENEFICIARY] doesn't lose this home if something happened to you. That hasn't changed in the last two minutes, has it?"
Wait for the yes. First micro-commitment.
Step 3 · Isolate it to the number
"So if it's not about understanding it — and it's not about whether [BENEFICIARY] matters — then really, it's just the number. Fair to say?"
Let them answer. Second micro-commitment. Now the real objection is named.
Step 4 · Solve it & assumptive close
"Then here's the good news — you're in control of that number, not me. We don't have to start where I showed you. Let's get [BENEFICIARY] set up today at something that feels easy, not stressful. The mistake people make is waiting for the 'perfect' amount — and [BENEFICIARY] ends up with zero in the meantime. Something in place beats nothing in place every time. We agree on that, right?"
"Perfect. We'll start there and bump it up at your review down the road." "You want the policy mailed to your home, or emailed to you?"
That last line is an assumptive close — you've moved them from price to delivery. Stop talking, take the answer, go to App.

⚡ Always Accessible
Objections
An objection is not a no — it's a request for clarity. Acknowledge → Neutralize → Redirect to their why. Never argue. Never defend. Always move forward with a question.
Isolation Rule — Use Before Solving Any Closing Objection: "Is it that you don't want this at all — or you know you want it and it's just a matter of which option?" These need completely different responses. Skip this and you solve the wrong problem.
⚡ Hail Mary Close — Still Resistant After Full Presentation
When to use: Three things must be true to close: product makes sense, fits budget, they trust you. Diagnose which one is missing — then fix that one thing.
"I want to be straight with you — we've gone through everything together and I want to make sure I'm not missing something on my end. Let me check three things real quick."
"One — the coverage needs to make sense. You said you really liked [OPTION], right?"
"Yes."
"Two — it needs to fit the budget. You mentioned [AMOUNT] might work, which means [LOWER AMOUNT] is even less — that should definitely be manageable, correct?"
"Yes, correct."
"Three — you have to trust the policy. [CARRIER] is A-rated, over 100 years in business — I'm assuming that's not the issue. Is there any discomfort with me specifically?"

"Okay — so since all three of those things exist… I'm just curious. What's really going on?"
Silence. Stop talking. First person to speak loses. They will fill the gap — and whatever comes out is the real objection you can actually handle.

Fallback Only
Appointment Booking
Only use when you genuinely cannot one-call close. Always go for the one-call first. Never book more than 24 hours out. Never book a one-legger.
⚠ One-Legger Rule: Never book without confirming the spouse or decision-maker will be present. A one-legger almost always reschedules or cancels.
