⚡ Objections Master
Phase 1 · Early Call
Intro Objections
These hit in the first 60 seconds. Default opener for nearly every one: "Perfect — that's actually the reason for my call. I see that on your file." Validate → neutralize → redirect back to their why. Never argue.
Objection "I'm not interested."
What's really happening Reflex resistance — they haven't thought about it at all. Don't fight it. Acknowledge and immediately redirect to consequence. Three escalation levels before moving on.
1ST
"Understood. I'll get the file updated here. Just so you're aware — this is the only coverage that pays the home loan if you become sick or pass away. What does it look like for your family to pay the bills without your income?"
2ND
"I'm assuming at the time you submitted this request you just wanted to make sure the home was taken care of for you and your family — God forbid if you become sick, disabled, or pass away. Is that fair?"
3RD
"Okay — are you more concerned that the price is going to be too high, or that you might not qualify?"
If Price Too High
"I see that on your file — looks like you were quoted some pretty steep numbers by one of my sales reps. I'm the manager for the [County/City] area and I've been assigned to find more practical, affordable options that fit your budget."
If Declined Before
"I see that on your file. I'm the senior medical underwriter who's been assigned to find options you will qualify for."
→ After 3 objections — move on. Abundance mindset.
Objection "I don't remember filling that out." / "I didn't fill anything out."
What's really happening Aged leads forget. This is not a real objection — it's confusion. Disarm it with humor, then keep moving.
"Did maybe your spouse or a family member fill it out?… No? That's okay — honestly, if you're anything like me, I can't even remember what I had for breakfast. This will be quick — go ahead and grab a pen and paper and we can knock this out right now."
Objection "I already talked to someone about this."
What's really happening They may have gotten a bad pitch or incomplete info. Use the file framing to re-establish your authority without disputing them.
"That makes sense — it looks like they didn't cover the proper information with you. They have me as the Senior Underwriter go over the details and close out the file properly."
Objection "Who do you work for exactly?"
What's really happening Trust check. Answer with authority — not defensiveness. The carrier breadth is your credibility.
"Great question. I'm actually an independent underwriter licensed through the state of [State]. What that means is I'm not captive to just one company — I work with the top 30 A and A+ rated carriers in the state, including Mutual of Omaha, National Life Group, Transamerica, and others. Does that make sense?"
Objection "This sounds like a scam."
What's really happening They've been burned before or are cautious. Don't get defensive — redirect to consequence immediately, offer to verify.
"It's not a scam to cover your mortgage loan with a life insurance policy. What will your family do if something happens to you and they no longer have your income? Mortgage protection pays the home loan if you become sick or pass away. This will only take 10 minutes. I can also verify my license through the state right now if that would help. Is morning or afternoon better for you?"
Objection "Is this required?"
What's really happening They're looking for an out. Act slightly confused — like the question is unexpected. Don't validate the frame.
"I mean… technically they won't take your home away if you don't have it… but I will tell you — you'd be one of the rare people without it in place. It's like driving a car without car insurance. Does that make sense?"
Objection "Take me off whatever list you have."
What's really happening They think this is spam. Reframe immediately — this is their own request, not a cold call.
"To be straight with you — there is no list. This is the request you submitted regarding protecting your family God forbid you were to get cancer, become disabled, or pass away. Anyway — the address we have on file for the home is [Address]?"
Objection "I'm busy right now." / "I'm at work." / "I'm driving."
What's really happening Friction objection — not a real no. Project busy-expert status. Mirror their urgency, don't back off.
Busy / At Work
"I'm really busy as well — I have a lot of families to get to. I'll make this brief. Go ahead and grab something to write with so we can knock this out."
Driving
"I don't want you to get in an accident — go ahead and pull over real quick and I'll get your information pulled up."
Objection "Can you just email / text it to me?"
What's really happening Either browsing or had a bad experience. Diagnose which before pushing back. Pull them back into emotion.
"Yeah, I can definitely send something — but so I don't send you generic stuff that doesn't fit, let me ask real quick: are you looking mainly to cover your family, or something more basic for final expenses?"
If They Keep Pushing
"I get it — the only reason I don't just blast quotes is because people overpay or get the wrong policy that doesn't pay out the way they think. If I send something, I want it to actually be right for you. Let me ask this real quick — if something happened, who would be the one stuck dealing with everything?"
Hard Close — Still Resisting
"Fair enough. Usually when someone asks me to just send info, it's because they're either just browsing — or they've had a bad experience before. Which one is it for you?"
Objection "How much is this going to cost?" / "Just give me a ballpark."
What's really happening They're trying to qualify it before investing time. Give a range to neutralize it, then pull them back into discovery.
"Yeah, I can give you a range — most people I help are somewhere between about $40 and $160 a month depending on age and health. But here's the thing — I don't want to throw you a number that ends up being wrong. This really comes down to how much coverage you need, how long you want it, and what you qualify for health-wise. Real quick — if something happened to you, would you want the house completely paid off for your family, or just the payments covered for a period of time?"
If They Push for Exact Price
"I hear you — I just don't want to misquote you. Give me two quick answers and I'll dial it in way closer for you. Fair enough?"
If Short or Resistant
"Sounds like you're just trying to see if this is even in the budget, right? If we found something in that range that protects the house — would you at least want to look at it?"
Objection "Are you trying to sell me something?"
What's really happening They're bracing for a pitch. Disarm the frame completely. You're closing a file, not selling.
"No — my job as the Senior Underwriter is to cover the proper information with you so we can close out the file and get this mailed to you. Sound fair?"
Objection "I think I saw an ad for this."
What's really happening They're thinking of accidental coverage ads. Clarify the difference and get them to self-select.
"Yes — that's typically for accidental coverage, which only pays out if you die in an accident. Were you looking for accidental-only, or did you want something that covers you for any cause of death?"
"I want something for any cause of death."
→ They've now self-qualified. Continue your normal process.
Phase 2 · At the Finish Line
Closing Objections
These hit after you've presented options and asked for the decision. Isolate before you solve. Is it doubt about the product, or doubt about the option? These are two different conversations.
Close Stall "I need to think about it."
What's really happening The most important question here is: do they not want it at all, or do they want it but can't decide which option? These need completely different responses. Isolate this first — every time.
"No problem — I get that. Most people like to think things through. I'm the same way."
"Just to recap — you mentioned that if something happened to you, [Beneficiary] would be in a really difficult spot. Was that accurate?"
"Just so I understand — is it more that you're unsure about whether to do this at all… or do you already know you want to and it's just a matter of which option makes the most sense?"
They Know They Want It — Deciding on Option
"That makes sense. Here's what works for a lot of people in your situation — we start with a lower option, just to see what you actually qualify for. Then when I follow up in a few days, once you've had time to think it through, we can adjust up or down. That way you're not overcommitting — but you're also not leaving things unprotected in the meantime. Does that sound fair?"
They Still Don't Want It
→ Go back into the why. Dig into their pain points. What specific scenario did they tell you about in discovery? Use that.
Close Stall "It's too expensive." / "I can't afford this right now."
What's really happening Either genuine budget concern or they don't see enough value yet. Flip the cost frame — doing nothing is the more expensive decision. Then downsize to something that fits.
"I understand — and I wouldn't want to put anything in place that you're not comfortable with."
"But based on what you told me earlier about your family — doing nothing would actually be the more expensive decision if something happened, right?"
(Pause. Let that land.)
"So instead of forcing a bigger plan, let's just put something smaller in place that fits the budget. You're covered starting now, and we can always adjust it later. Sound fair?"
"Yeah, that makes sense."
Frame shift The premium isn't the cost — the mortgage that goes unpaid is the cost. Once they accept that frame, price stops being the conversation.
Close Stall "I need to talk to my spouse first."
What's really happening Often a stall, not a real blocker. Isolate first — same as "need to think about it." If spouse isn't present, either get them on the call or use the annual review bridge to start low.
"Okay — do you need to think about whether you want the coverage at all, or do you already know you want it and it's just a matter of which option to go with?"
They Want It — Just Deciding
"Completely understandable. Here's what I'd recommend — let's start at the low end with what you know you can afford. You're actually going to hear from me twice: once today, and then again about a year from now for your annual review. At that point, if you want to bump up the coverage, you absolutely can. Anyway — the address we have on file to mail the policy to is [Address], right?"
Close Stall "Do I have to make a decision today?"
What's really happening They want permission to delay. Don't give it — but make it easy by starting small. Yes is the answer, but make it painless.
"Yes — we do need to submit the original coverage request today. However, since most people need a little time to look over their finances and decide on an option, what I'd recommend is starting at the low end with what you know you can comfortably afford. If you want to bump it up, just give me a call or send a text and I can adjust it. And I do annual reviews with all my clients — so we'll revisit this a year from now as well. Anyway — the address we have on file to mail the policy to is [Address], right?"
Last Resort Still getting resistance after full presentation
What's really happening Three things need to be true to close: product makes sense, fits budget, they trust you. Diagnose which one is missing.
"I want to be straight with you — I let you know at the front end of the call that we'd need to come to some kind of conclusion today. Let me check three things real quick."
"One — the coverage needs to make sense. You said you really liked [Option], right?"
"Yes."
"Two — it needs to fit the budget. You mentioned [Amount] might work, which means [Lower Amount] is less — that should definitely work, correct?"
"Yes, correct."
"Three — you have to trust the policy. [Carrier] is A-rated, so I'm assuming that's not the issue… Is there any discomfort with me specifically?"
"No, not at all."
"Okay — so since all three of those things exist, I'm just curious… what's really going on?"
(Silence. Let them fill the gap.)
Phase 3 · Coverage Confusion
Coverage & Competition
These objections come from clients who think they're already covered. Your job is to expose the gap — not argue with them. The policy flip sequence is where you pick up extra deals.
Objection "I already have coverage." / "I already took care of this."
What's really happening Could be a genuine policy, homeowners insurance confusion, or a brush-off. Diagnose which before responding. The policy flip drill below is where you find out.
If They Have an Actual Life / Mortgage Policy — Run the Flip
"Perfect — that's the reason for my call. I see that on your file here. Just to confirm I'm speaking with the right person — the address we have on file for the policy is [Address]? And I have your date of birth listed as [DOB]?"
"The reason I'm reaching out is that it looks like one of our junior underwriters helped you with coverage but didn't update your file properly."
Run through the policy flip checklist:
  • Did you receive the policy packet in the mail okay?
  • Did you go with the complete plan or the partial plan?
  • How much coverage did you end up with?
  • What are you paying per month?
  • Which company did you go with?
  • Is there a reason you went with one of our B-rated carriers?
  • Were you not able to qualify for one of our top-rated options? Any health issues?
After Flip Checklist Look up quotes. If you can get them better coverage or a lower rate — make the switch. If it's a term policy without living benefits or cash value — build value on what they're missing. Then: "This will only take a few minutes and we can get this updated for you."
If They're Confusing It with Homeowners Insurance
"Has anyone explained the difference between homeowners insurance and mortgage protection to you before?"
"Your homeowner's policy covers physical damage — fire, flood, storm. Mortgage protection is what pays the home loan for you and your family if you become sick or pass away. Does that make sense?"
Objection "I already have life insurance through work."
What's really happening They think employer coverage = solved. Two angles: (1) it disappears when the job does, (2) less than 2% of work policies ever actually pay out due to stipulations. Use both.
"That's great to have. Your work life insurance pays out when you pass away. Mortgage protection is different — it not only pays the home loan when you pass, but also while you're still living if you become sick and can't work. Did anyone explain that to you?"
"Also — the moment you leave, retire, or change jobs, all of that work coverage disappears. And worst case — if you were to get cancer or a serious illness and then get laid off, you'd lose those benefits right when you need them most. At that point, you likely wouldn't be able to qualify for new coverage health-wise."
"Less than 2% of work policies ever actually pay out because of very specific stipulations. To get it to pay, you'd typically need a work-related death. And if you're absent from work for more than 90 consecutive days before you pass, it doesn't have to pay out at all. For that reason, most of my clients have a private policy as well. Is that the case for you?"
Objection "We have life insurance — we don't need mortgage protection."
What's really happening They don't understand the living benefits distinction. This is an education play. The 89% stat is your strongest tool here.
"Has anyone explained the difference between mortgage protection and life insurance to you before?"
"Both mortgage protection and life insurance pay out as a check directly to your beneficiary when you pass away. The reason mortgage protection is a stronger product is because it also covers you on the living side — not just the death side."
"What that means is — any situation where you get sick, become disabled, have cancer, a heart attack, a stroke, or any other critical, chronic, or terminal illness — this policy pays out to supplement that loss of income and cover medical bills."
"The number one cause of bankruptcies and home foreclosures in this country is not death. It's unforeseen medical bills, or becoming disabled and not being able to work. You actually have an 89% higher likelihood of becoming sick or disabled than you do of passing away. That's why our clients love this coverage so much. Does that make sense?"
Objection "Are you with my bank?" / "The bank said I don't need this." / "I already have this with the bank."
What's really happening They're confusing PMI or bank-sold mortgage insurance with what you offer. Clear the confusion fast — 2009 rule change is your credibility anchor.
Are You With the Bank?
"No — I'm licensed through the Department of Insurance. This coverage used to be done at the bank up until about 2009. The problem was the bank was the beneficiary of the policy — not your family. The rules changed. Now it's done through state underwriters like myself, so you have private insurance and the money stays with your family. Does that make sense?"
I Already Have This with the Bank
"That's a common misconception. What you have with the bank is Private Mortgage Insurance — that protects the bank's investment if you default on the loan. Mortgage protection is what pays the loan for you and your family if you become sick or pass away. Does that make sense?"
Objection "We took care of all this at closing." / "We don't have that lender anymore."
What's really happening They think the lender handled it. Use the 2008 change + file framing to reposition without arguing.
Took Care of at Closing
"Correct — you took care of your mortgage at closing. But I'm calling in regards to the mortgage protection — that's different. The banks used to offer this before 2008, but now it's contracted out directly to the insurance carriers, which is who I work with. It's my job as the underwriter licensed with the state to run it through the various companies and figure out which product makes the most sense for you and your family. Does that make sense?"
Don't Have That Lender Anymore
"Correct — that's exactly the reason for my call. I see that on your file, which is probably why it's still showing as pending and slipped through the cracks on our end. I apologize for that. Just to confirm I'm speaking with the right person — the address we have on file for the mortgage is [Address]? So as you may recall, this is the coverage that takes care of the home — God forbid you become sick, disabled, or pass away. Does that make sense?"
Objection "I already have an agent I'm working with."
What's really happening Loyalty or they're in process with someone else. Don't compete — just earn the right to a second look.
"Totally fair — most people I speak with already have someone. Out of curiosity — what made you decide to start looking into this in the first place?"
(Reopen the conversation, then:)
"That makes sense. Are you just getting a second opinion, or are you pretty set on moving forward with them?"
Moving Forward with Other Agent
"Got it — and I'm not here to replace anyone. What I've found though is a lot of the time what people were shown doesn't fully line up with what they actually needed — especially with mortgage protection. Would it hurt to take 10–15 minutes just to make sure everything is structured the right way before you lock anything in?"
The Mechanic Analogy "I actually recommend all my clients speak with more than one person. It's like getting a car repaired — you generally don't go with the first quote. You compare cost and diagnosis from different mechanics. With something as important as your life and health coverage, you want to make sure you have the best benefits at the lowest cost possible."
Objection "We're planning to sell the home." / "We already sold it."
Planning to Sell
"Perfect — this coverage actually travels with you. It transfers when you buy your next home. Are you planning to stay local or moving out of state?"
Already Sold — Bought New Home
"Perfect — that's probably why your file was showing as flagged and incomplete. What's the new address? I'll go ahead and update that for you."
Already Sold — Now Renting / Home Paid Off
"Gotcha — it looks like that's why your file was flagged here. We also handle life insurance and personal protection that covers you if you were to become sick, disabled, or pass away. Have you gotten that set up yet?"
Objection "I've tried this before and I don't qualify."
What's really happening They got declined by a captive agent with limited carriers. Your 30-carrier access is your differentiator.
"That's exactly why I'm calling. It looks like you spoke with third-party vendors who have limited access to carriers. As a Senior Underwriter, I work with all 30 carriers in [State] that handle this coverage. The only reason you'd be declined is active cancer — is that the case for you?"
Phase 4 · Niche Pushback
Specialty Objections
These hit less frequently but require specific knowledge. VA, TSP, disability, savings, probate. Know these cold — aged leads skew older and military households are common.
Specialty "The VA has me covered." / "I have VGLI."
What's really happening They're confusing the VA loan guarantee (protects the bank) with personal life coverage. Two separate things. The living benefits gap is your strongest angle.
"Your VGLI coverage can go up to $400,000 — but it's not tied to your mortgage balance, and as costs increase it becomes harder to keep long-term. VGLI also doesn't come with critical, chronic, or terminal illness benefits. So if you had cancer, a stroke, a heart attack, or became unable to work — VGLI doesn't pay anything while you're still alive."
"The VA backs the loan for the bank — but it does nothing for you and your family if something happens. That's exactly why mortgage protection exists separately — to make sure the home loan gets paid for your family if you become sick or pass away."
Key Distinction VA loan = financing vehicle. Protects the bank. VGLI = life coverage, no living benefits, not tied to mortgage balance, costs increase with age. Mortgage protection fills the living benefits gap.
Specialty "I have a TSP — I think that covers it."
What's really happening They're treating retirement savings as insurance. The framing is wrong — they'd have to pull from retirement to cover a gap that shouldn't exist.
"That's good — most people I talk to have a TSP. Just so I understand — that's mainly your retirement account, right?"
"Got it. And that's more for building money over time — not really something your family can immediately use to pay off the house if something happened tomorrow. Correct?"
"That's the only difference here — this is designed to make sure the mortgage is handled right away so your family doesn't have to figure it out later."
Push Back — "It Has a Lot of Money In It"
"That's a good thing — totally. But if something happened unexpectedly, would you want your family having to pull from your retirement… or would it be better if the house was already taken care of and that money stayed intact?"
Brushing You Off
"Sounds like you've done a good job setting things up. I'm just helping people make sure one specific gap is covered. If we could handle the mortgage for your family without touching your TSP — would that be worth looking at?"
Specialty "I have disability coverage through work."
What's really happening Disability handles income. Mortgage protection handles the mortgage specifically. These are two separate problems.
"That makes sense — most employer plans help with income, not the mortgage itself. Let me ask you something real quick — if you couldn't work, does that disability plan pay 100% of your income and guarantee the house is paid off in its final years?"
(They'll usually say no.)
"So it sounds like disability handles income — and this handles the mortgage itself. Would you agree?"
Specialty "We have savings." / "Our retirement would cover it."
What's really happening They're planning to liquidate long-term assets for a short-term crisis. Walk them down the road of what that actually looks like.
"How long could you realistically live off savings?"
"And once that runs out — what happens next?"
"If something happened unexpectedly — would you want your family having to drain their retirement savings just to stay in the house? Or would it be better if the mortgage was already handled?"
Specialty "My family will step in and help."
What's really happening They're outsourcing the burden to people who love them. One line flips the entire frame.
"That's great that you have family like that. But if you really think about it — would you rather leave them with a check… or a bill?"
(Pause. Let it land.)
Knowledge Client doesn't understand why a payout matters quickly — use when they downplay the urgency
Use this proactively Don't wait for them to ask. Drop this when they're soft on urgency — it makes the consequence real.
"Let me explain something most families don't realize. When someone passes away — if money or property isn't set up properly — everything usually has to go through something called probate court. A judge steps in and oversees who gets what, what debts get paid, and how everything is distributed. That process can take 6 months… sometimes 12… sometimes longer depending on the state."
"During that time, the mortgage payment doesn't stop."
(Pause.)
"The bank still expects their money. So if the house is tied up in probate and payments aren't being made — what usually happens?"
Specialty "I'm young and healthy — I don't need it yet."
What's really happening They think health is a reason to wait. Flip it — health is exactly the reason to act now.
"Honestly — that's exactly who qualifies for the best coverage."
(Pause.)
"You being healthy is the reason to do it — not the reason to wait. Most people who end up paying double for coverage were healthy when they first thought about it. The healthiest people get the best plans. The sickest people get the most expensive ones."
Specialty "Do you have anything cheaper?"
What's really happening They want to cut cost without understanding what they're cutting. The parachute analogy reframes cheap as dangerous.
"When you jump out of a plane — do you want the cheapest parachute or the best one? If you just want cheap life insurance, I can certainly help with that. But you mentioned that [Cash Value / Living Benefits] was important to you — is that still the case?"
Phase 5 · Compliance

Phase 6 · Study Mode
Deep Knowledge
Study these to freestyle when a call goes off-script. Memorize the psychology behind each objection — not just the line. These are the concepts that let you handle anything.
Core Principles
The 4-Step Objection Framework 1. Acknowledge & move on — never argue. 2. Memorize all objections until second nature. 3. Maintain control — you're the expert. 4. Always end with a question — whoever asks the most questions controls the conversation.
The Isolation Rule Before solving any closing objection, ask: "Is it that you don't want this at all — or you know you want it and you're just deciding which option?" These need completely different responses. Skipping this step costs you deals.
The Abundance Rule If someone gives you more than 3 objections in a row — move on. Not every lead converts. The mindset is there are enough people who will buy. Don't beg.
Psychology Deep Dives
Concept Why "I Talked to the Bank — I Don't Need It" Always Has an Answer
"Right — the bank doesn't require it. The bank just requires the payment. But if something happened to you, the bank still expects that payment — correct?"
"That's actually why most families put this in place — not because the bank needs it, but because the family needs the house protected."
Concept The "Probably" Pattern Interrupt
When to use Whenever they use the word "probably" in reference to their family being okay. Repeat the word back. Forces commitment or surfaces the real hesitation.
They say: "They'd probably be okay."
"Probably?"
(Pause.)
"So you're not 100% positive?"
This forces them to either commit or reveal the hesitation. One of the most powerful one-word follow-ups in the playbook.
Concept Work Insurance — The Full Picture
Three separate problems with work insurance — know all three:
Problem 1 — It Disappears
Coverage ends the moment you leave, retire, change jobs, or get laid off.
Problem 2 — It Rarely Pays Out
Less than 2% of work policies ever pay out. Most require a work-related death. If you're absent for more than 90 consecutive days before passing — it doesn't pay at all.
Problem 3 — No Living Benefits
Work insurance only pays on death. Mortgage protection pays on death AND on disability, critical illness, chronic illness, or terminal diagnosis. The living benefits gap is what makes our product categorically different.
Concept The 89% Stat — Know How to Deploy It
When to use After you've established the living benefits distinction. This is the close on the "we have life insurance" objection.
"The number one cause of bankruptcies and home foreclosures in this country is not death. It's unforeseen medical bills, or becoming disabled and not being able to work. You actually have an 89% higher likelihood of becoming sick or disabled than you do of passing away."
Concept The Healthy Person's Window — Build Urgency Before the Close
When to use When they're healthy but stalling. This reframes health as a reason to act — not a reason to wait.
"When you apply for coverage, you're really getting two things. First — the policy itself. And second — your medical approval. That's actually the most important part."
"Right now, you're still in what we call the 'healthy person's window.' Windows open and they close. You're still healthy enough to qualify for preferred coverage — and that gets harder every single year."
"But here's the thing — if you get approved today and something happens tomorrow, it doesn't matter. They cannot take your medical approval away from you. Your rate and your benefit are locked in. Does that make sense?"

Hard Close When they're on the edge but won't commit
"Are you willing to go one more day without coverage if something happened tomorrow?"
"Are you okay with that happening to you?"
"So if we didn't have anything at all for tomorrow — what does that situation look like for your family?"
